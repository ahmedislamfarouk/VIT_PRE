Text-to-Video Generation
1. Introduction to Text-to-Video Generation
Text-to-video (T2V) generation is one of the most ambitious frontiers in generative AI. Given a
natural language description, these models synthesize coherent, temporally consistent video
sequences that visually reflect the content of the input text. This task is substantially harder than
text-to-image generation because video introduces an additional temporal dimension: not only
must individual frames be realistic and semantically aligned with the prompt, but consecutive
frames must maintain visual consistency, smooth motion, and logical scene progression.
A video generation model works by turning a text prompt into a structured representation using
a text encoder, then starting from random noise and refining it step by step through a denoising
network. A scheduler guides this process, while encoders and decoders move between pixel
space and a compressed latent space for efficiency. Unlike image models, video models
process 3D tokens that capture both spatial detail and temporal motion. Because decoding
requires a lot of memory, many pipelines use frame-by-frame decoding to make generation
more efficient.
1.1 Core Challenges
• Temporal Consistency: Frames must form a coherent motion sequence without
flickering, object drifting, or inconsistent lighting.
• Semantic Alignment: Every visual element in the generated video must faithfully reflect
the semantics of the text prompt.
• Motion Realism: Physics, dynamics, and natural motion must be plausibly reproduced
even for complex actions.
• Long-Range Coherence: Objects and scenes must remain consistent across dozens or
hundreds of frames.
• Computational Cost: Generating even short, high-resolution video clips demands
extremely computed resources.

2. Default Architecture of Text-to-Video Models
Modern text-to-video models converge on a shared architectural blueprint. While individual
implementations differ, the following pipeline represents the canonical architecture used by
leading T2V systems.
2.1 The Canonical Pipeline
Five-Stage Text-to-Video Pipeline
Stage 1 → Text Encoder (Language Model)
Stage 2 → Cross-Attention Conditioning
Stage 3 → Spatiotemporal Latent Diffusion (UNet or DiT)
Stage 4 → Temporal Attention / 3D Convolutions
Stage 5 → Video Decoder (VAE / Pixel Upsampler)
2.1.1 Text Encoder
The text encoder transforms a natural language prompt into a rich, high-dimensional embedding
vector that captures semantic meaning. Modern T2V systems predominantly use large pre-trained
language models such as:
• CLIP (Contrastive Language–Image Pre-Training): Provides joint text-image
representations, excellent for aligning visual outputs with textual descriptions. Used in
early diffusion video models.
• T5 / T5-XXL: A powerful sequence-to-sequence encoder from Google. Produces longer,
richer token sequences. Used in Imagen Video, CogVideoX, and Wan 2.1.
• LLAMA / GPT-style encoders: Some recent models leverage decoder-only LLMs as
text encoders, benefiting from deep world knowledge.
2.1.2 Latent Space & VAE
Operating directly in pixel space for video is computationally intractable. All state-of-the-art T2V
models encode frames (or video clips) into a compressed latent space using a Variational
Autoencoder (VAE). The VAE reduces spatial resolution (e.g., 8× down sampling per axis) and
temporal resolution, dramatically cutting the number of tokens that the diffusion model must
process. After generations, the VAE decoder reconstructs the latent representation back to full-
resolution video frames.

2.1.3 Spatiotemporal Denoising Backbone
This is the core generative component. Given noisy latent video tensors and the text conditioning
signal, the backbone iteratively denoises to produce a clean latent video. Two dominant
architectures exist:
A) 3D UNet
Extends the standard 2D UNet with temporal convolutions and temporal attention layers
interleaved between spatial operations. Each 3D convolutional block processes spatial and
temporal dimensions jointly. Cross-attention layers inject the text embedding at every resolution
level. This design was dominant in early diffusion T2V models (e.g., Make-A-Video, Imagen
Video).
B) Diffusion Transformer (DiT)
Replaces the convolutional UNet with a pure Transformer operating on video patch tokens
("pacification" of latent video into 3D spacetime tokens). Each token represents a spatiotemporal
region. Multi-head self-attention operates over all tokens simultaneously, capturing long-range
dependencies across both space and time. Text is injected via cross-attention or as additional
conditioning tokens. DiTs have shown superior scaling and generation quality, and represent the
current frontier (e.g., Sora, CogVideoX, Wan 2.1, HunyuanVideo).
2.1.4 Diffusion Process
Text-to-video models universally adopt Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Models (DDPMs) or their
variants (DDIM, Flow Matching). The forward process gradually adds Gaussian noise to latent
video tensors across T timesteps. The reverse (generative) process learns to remove noise
conditioned on the text embedding. Flow Matching (used in Wan 2.1 and others) replaces the
DDPM noise schedule with a direct flow from noise to data, offering faster inference and improved
stability.
2.1.5 Temporal Modeling Strategies
Handling time is the defining challenge of video generation. Four main strategies are employed:
• Temporal Self-Attention: Attention computed across the time dimension, allowing each
frame to attend to all other frames. Expensive but effective for global consistency.
• 3D Convolutional Layers: Factorized or full 3D convolutions capture local
spatiotemporal patterns. Efficient but limited in receptive field.
• Causal Attention / Causal 3D VAE: For autoregressive T2V models, attention is
masked so each frame only attends to prior frames, enabling video extension and
streaming generation.
• Full Spacetime Attention (Full 3D Attention): All spatial and temporal tokens attend
each other. Maximum expressiveness but highest memory cost. Used in Hornyhanded
and Wan 2.1.

2.2 Training Objectives
Most T2V models are trained using a denoising score-matching objective: the model learns to
predict the added noise (or the clean data directly via v-prediction or flow matching) at each
diffusion timestep. Training datasets typically include hundreds of millions of video-text pairs
scraped from the web, cleaned with caption quality filters and NSFW removal. Classifier-Free
Guidance (CFG) is universally used to improve prompt adherence: the model is trained jointly
with and without text conditioning, and at inference time, outputs are guided toward the conditional
direction.
3. Major Text-to-Video Models
3.1 Sora (OpenAI, 2024)
Sora represented a watershed moment in T2V generation. It is a Diffusion Transformer trained
on a massive dataset of internet video, capable of generating up to 60-second clips at 1080p with
unprecedented temporal coherence. Sora introduced the concept of "spacetime patches" —
treating video as a sequence of 3D patches across space and time — enabling the model to
process variable-length, variable-resolution videos natively within a single unified architecture.
• Architecture: Diffusion Transformer (DiT) on spacetime latent patches.
• Text Encoder: GPT-4-level language model for rich prompt understanding.
• Resolution: Up to 1920×1080, variable aspect ratios.
• Duration: Up to 60 seconds.
• Key Innovation: Spacetime patch tokenization; video continuation and editing
capabilities.
• Availability: Limited API access; not fully open-source.
3.2 Wan 2.1
Wan 2.1 is currently one of the most capable open-source text-to-video models available. It
employs a Flow Matching objective on a 3D Full Attention Diffusion Transformer architecture,
enabling high-quality video generation across both text-to-video and image-to-video tasks. A key
innovation is its 3D Causal VAE, which encodes video with temporal causality, reducing the latent
dimensionality while preserving fine motion detail.
• Architecture: DiT with Full 3D Attention; Flow Matching training.
• Text Encoder: Multilingual T5-XXL encoder.
• Resolution: Up to 1280×720 at 24 fps.
• Duration: Up to ~8 seconds per clip (extendable).
• Key Innovation: 3D Causal VAE; multilingual support; open weights released.
• Availability: Open-source (Apache 2.0 / custom license).

3.3 Imagen Video (Google DeepMind, 2022–2023)
Imagen Video was an early landmark model from Google, extending the Imagen text-to-image
system with a video diffusion cascaded pipeline. It used a series of spatial and temporal super-
resolution models, starting from small low-framerate keyframes and progressively upsampling in
both space and time.
• Architecture: Cascaded 3D UNet diffusion pipeline (7 models in sequence).
• Text Encoder: T5-XXL (frozen).
• Resolution: Up to 1280×768 through cascaded upsampling.
• Duration: ~3.5 seconds.
• Key Innovation: Video diffusion cascades; joint training across T2I and T2V.
• Availability: Research paper only; not publicly released.
3.4 Veo3
Veo 3 is Google’s state-of-the-art video generation model, producing high-fidelity, 8-second
clips at 720p or 1080p (16:9) with native, always-on audio at 24fps. Available via the Gemini
API, it excels across dialogue-driven scenes, cinematic realism, and creative animation,
capturing quoted dialogue, explicit sound effects, and ambient soundscapes directly from your
prompt.
Veo 3 translates text prompts into cinematic shots with coherent lighting, depth-of-field, and
filmic color, while maintaining temporal consistency frame to frame. It natively renders
synchronized dialogue, SFX, and ambience, enabling lip-aware speech, scene-matched
acoustics, and timing-accurate cues that elevate realism.
• Architecture Diffusion Transformer (DiT) + Latent Space
• Text Encoder LLM-based (Gemini-like)
• Resolution 720p / 1080p
• Duration ~8 seconds
• Key Innovation Native audio + cinematic realism + lip-sync
• Availability Gemini API

4. GANs for Video Generation: Why They Fall Short
4.1 The GAN Approach to Video
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) were the first class of deep generative models to
demonstrate convincing image synthesis, and researchers naturally attempted to extend them to
video generation. The core GAN paradigm pits two neural networks against each other: a
Generator (G) that synthesizes fake samples, and a Discriminator (D) that distinguishes between
real and generated samples. Training converges when G can reliably fool D.
Early video GAN architectures such as VideoGAN (2016), MoCoGAN (2018), DVD-GAN (2019),
and TGAN attempted to generate short, low-resolution video clips by adding temporal
convolutional layers or recurrent components to image GANs. Some innovations included:
• Content-Motion Decomposition: Separate latent spaces for static scene content and
temporal motion (MoCoGAN).
• Dual Discriminators: Separate spatial and temporal discriminators operating on
individual frames and temporal windows.
• Progressive Growing: Starting from low resolution and progressively increasing,
adapted from ProGAN.
4.2 Fundamental Limitations of GANs for Text-to-Video
Despite early promise, GANs exhibit several deep structural limitations that make them poorly
suited for high-quality, text-conditioned video generation. These limitations are not mere
engineering challenges — they reflect fundamental properties of the adversarial training
paradigm.
4.2.1 Training Instability
GAN training is notoriously unstable due to the adversarial minimax game. The training objective
requires a delicate balance between G and D: if D becomes too strong, gradients to G vanish (the
vanishing gradient problem); if D is too weak, G receives insufficient signal to improve. For video
generation, this instability is amplified because the discriminator must evaluate consistency
across both spatial and temporal dimensions simultaneously, making saddle-point convergence
far harder to achieve.
4.2.2 Mode Collapse
Mode collapse is the most notorious failure mode of GANs: the generator converges to producing
only a narrow subset of the target data distribution, ignoring vast regions of the true distribution.
For video generation, this manifests as limited diversity — the model may generate only specific
types of motions, scenes, or visual styles, regardless of the input text prompt. Conditional GANs
can partially mitigate this with conditioning on text embeddings, but mode collapse at the video
level is extremely difficult to fully eliminate, especially for open-domain text conditioning.

4.2.3 Temporal Inconsistency
GANs lack a principled mechanism for ensuring long-range temporal consistency. While temporal
discriminators penalize frame-level inconsistencies within their temporal window, they cannot
enforce global scene consistency over dozens of seconds. Video GANs systematically exhibit
flickering, identity drift (objects change appearance between frames), and physically implausible
motion over time. Diffusion models, by contrast, generate the entire latent video jointly during the
denoising process, naturally enforcing global coherence.
4.2.4 Poor Text-Video Alignment
Aligning complex, nuanced text prompts with generated video is fundamentally about learning
rich cross-modal representations. GANs condition on text by injecting embeddings into the
generator, but their adversarial training objective has no direct term that measures semantic
alignment between the text and the generated video. Diffusion models, trained with classifier-free
guidance and cross-attention, demonstrate far stronger semantic adherence because the
denoising objective directly rewards the model for producing samples that are consistent with the
conditioning signal at every noise level.
4.2.5 Resolution and Duration Scaling
Scaling GANs to high-resolution, long-duration video is computationally and architecturally
challenging. The discriminator must process the entire video at full resolution, leading to quadratic
or cubic memory scaling with spatial and temporal dimensions. Progressive training strategies
(like StyleGAN) are difficult to adapt to the temporal dimension. Diffusion models operate in a
compressed latent space via the VAE, dramatically reducing the effective dimensionality and
enabling generation of much higher-quality, longer videos within practical memory budgets.
4.2.6 Evaluation Difficulty
Because GANs directly optimize for fooling a discriminator rather than maximizing a well-defined
probability, it is difficult to measure the true quality of the distribution learned by the generator.
Metrics such as Fréchet Inception Distance (FID) and Fréchet Video Distance (FVD) are imperfect
proxies. Diffusion models optimize for a well-defined variational lower bound (or flow matching
objective), providing more principled training signals and more predictable improvements with
scale.

4.4 When GANs Are Still Relevant
It is important not to dismiss GANs entirely. They retain practical advantages in specific scenarios:
• Real-Time Applications: GANs generate samples in a single forward pass, making
them orders of magnitude faster than diffusion models at inference time. For real-time
video style transfer or live face synthesis, GANs remain relevant.
• Video-to-Video Translation: Architectures like vid2vid and Pix2Pix achieve strong
results for structured video translation tasks where temporal GAN discriminators are
highly effective.
• Adversarial Fine-Tuning: Some recent hybrid systems use GAN-like discriminators as
auxiliary losses to fine-tune diffusion models for sharper, more artifact-free outputs (e.g.,
adversarial diffusion distillation in SDXL-Turbo and similar approaches).
